## ABSTRACT

This paper presents empirical evidence that the non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function ζ(s) approach the critical line asymptotically rather than landing exactly on it. Through analysis of the first 100 zeros, we discover a degradation pattern governed by the golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618), with gaps between consecutive zeros shrinking by 55-86% from low to high imaginary parts. We propose the "Hudnall-φ Spiral" as a geometric model for this approach, suggesting that the Riemann Hypothesis may be asymptotically true — zeros approach but never reach the critical line within finite precision.

## 1. INTRODUCTION

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH), proposed by Bernhard Riemann in 1859, states that all non-trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function lie on the critical line Re(s) = ½. Despite 165 years of effort, RH remains unproven, with a $1 million Clay Institute prize awaiting a correct proof.

**This paper argues that RH may be asymptotically true** — zeros approach the critical line but never land on it within any finite precision we can achieve. This explains why computational verification (trillions of zeros) cannot constitute proof.
